Well Diagnostics

Diagnostics is the work we do before recommending any repair. If the water has stopped, the pressure is dropping, or the pump won't shut off, something is wrong, and the right move is to figure out what it is before buying parts. A diagnostic visit pressure-tests the system, checks the electrical side, and inspects the pressure switch and the pump itself. If the situation calls for it, we also run flow rate or static water level tests. The idea is to find the actual problem rather than guess and replace. When we're done, we explain what we found and what it will take to fix it.

Pressure Tank and Pressure Switch Services

Your pressure tank stores water under pressure so the pump doesn't have to cycle every time someone opens a faucet. The pressure switch is what tells the pump to turn on and off. When either one fails, the symptoms tend to look like pump problems. Short cycling. Pressure dropping when you run water. A pump that stays on constantly, or one that won't start at all. We service and replace both.

One note on terminology. Pressure tanks and bladder tanks are not the same component, even though homeowners sometimes use the terms interchangeably. If you already know you need a bladder tank replaced, that's listed separately in additional services below.

Well Pipe Repair

A failing pipe in a well system usually shows up in the water or the pressure. You might notice sediment where there wasn't any, pressure that keeps dropping, or a pump that keeps running without ever building up pressure. We repair the pipe work in the well equipment itself. That includes the drop pipe inside the well casing, the pitless adapter (the fitting that takes the water line from the well over to the house), and the above-ground connections at the pressure tank.

What we don't do is household plumbing. If the leak is inside the house, you need a plumber. In Florida, most well pipe failures come down to corrosion or mineral buildup, and both of those take years to become obvious before you notice a symptom.

Leak Detection

A leak in your well system is a different animal from a leak in the plumbing inside your house. Well-system leaks happen at the wellhead, at the pump housing, or around the fittings on the pressure tank. You'd usually notice them as dropping pressure, a pump that keeps running when nothing's being used, or a wet area of ground where there shouldn't be one.

We handle the equipment side. Wellhead, pump, tank, and the piping between them. Anything inside the house is a plumber's job. When the leak is at the well, we can find it and fix it.

Water Quality Analysis

On a private well, nobody is running water quality tests for you. That's the homeowner's job, and most homeowners don't realize it until they hit a situation that makes them start wondering what's actually in their water.

A water quality analysis looks at bacterial content, minerals like iron and calcium, and chemical indicators that might point to contamination from nearby land use, septic proximity, or runoff. We coordinate the testing through a certified lab and sit down with you afterward to go through the results together.

Well Service FAQs

How do I know if my well pressure tank needs to be replaced?
Short cycling is the most common warning sign. If your pump kicks on every time someone runs a faucet instead of letting the tank handle small draws, the tank is likely losing its ability to hold pressure. Other signs include fluctuating water pressure at the tap, or a tank that feels heavy all over when you knock on it rather than hollow near the top. In Florida conditions, pressure tanks typically last ten to fifteen years. A diagnostic visit can tell you whether the tank is actually what's failing, or whether the problem is somewhere else in the system.
What's the difference between well diagnostics and a well inspection?
Different services for different situations. Diagnostics is what happens when something is wrong with your system and you need to know what's causing it before spending money on parts. An inspection is a documented assessment, usually ordered during a real estate transaction, that tests the system end to end and produces the paperwork the closing needs. Diagnostics solves a problem. An inspection documents a condition.
How do I know if my well pump needs to be repaired or fully replaced?
There isn't one answer. The age of the pump matters, the specific component that's failing matters, and the cost of parts relative to a new unit matters. A twenty-year-old pump with a dead motor is almost always better replaced. A five-year-old pump with a bad pressure switch is a cheap repair. We diagnose first and then lay out honest options, including what kind of lifespan you can expect from each path.
Do I need a permit to have my well serviced or repaired in Florida?
Routine service and repair usually don't require a permit. Larger work [PLACEHOLDER — confirm with Danny: permit triggers] typically does. When a permit is needed, we file it as part of the work.
What contaminants are most common in Tampa Bay well water?
The usual suspects in this area are water hardness from calcium and magnesium, iron, and sulfur, which is what produces the rotten-egg smell some homeowners notice. Bacterial contamination shows up too, most often after flooding or after work has been done on the well itself. Some parts of the service area also see tannins from organic matter. Until you have an actual test, you're guessing. Once you have results, the fix depends on what the test finds. Softening handles hardness, filtration handles iron and sulfur, and bacterial issues need targeted treatment, which in our experience is usually hydrogen peroxide.
