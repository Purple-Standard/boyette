What Is a Submersible Water Pump?

A submersible water pump sits below the water line inside your well casing. Unlike a jet pump that pulls water from above ground, a submersible pushes water up from the bottom, which is more efficient and quieter. The motor is sealed to run underwater without damage.

Most residential wells in Florida use submersible pumps because the water table sits far enough below the surface that pulling from above is impractical. You will also find submersible pumps feeding irrigation systems, pressure tanks, and anywhere a steady water supply needs to move up and out of a deep source.

When a submersible pump fails, you notice fast. The water stops. Pressure drops. The pump cycles without getting anywhere, or faucets start sputtering air. These calls come in daily, and the problem almost always traces back to the pump itself, a control component, or the wiring that runs down to it.

Section 4 — Installation Process + Diagram

What Does Submersible Well Pump Installation Involve?

Installation is skilled work. Here is what the job looks like from the moment the truck pulls up.

Infographic — Installation Process Diagram
Step sequence showing the 6 installation phases: pump sizing, pulling the old pump, lowering the new pump, electrical connection (2-wire vs. 3-wire), pressure tank tie-in, system test. Format options: numbered icon strip OR cross-section diagram with numbered callouts (matches visual language of Section 2 infographic).

First we size the pump to the well. That means checking depth, flow rate, and household demand, then matching horsepower to the job. Undersized pumps burn out. Oversized pumps run inefficiently and wear down other components.

If we are replacing a failed pump, the old pump and drop pipe come out of the casing. The new pump goes in next. It attaches to drop pipe and a safety rope and lowers down to the target depth, with wiring secured alongside it on the way down.

Electrical connection depends on the pump. A 2-wire pump has the starting components built into the motor. A 3-wire pump runs through an above-ground control box that we wire in.

From there the system ties into your pressure tank and pressure switch. If either is worn, we flag it before finishing. We run the system, check the pressure cycles, verify flow at the fixtures, and make sure everything is behaving before we leave.
